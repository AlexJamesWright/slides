Compact object mergers



  • Best sources for GWs
  • Tests for GR, QCD, condensed matter physics
  • Responsible for producing heavy elements through rapid neutron capture (key for this project)












[credit: aLIGO]

Probably heard this intro slide before, so briefly: Compact mergers are the best sources for GWs. Both binary NS and BH mergers have been detected, and their results allow us to test theories that are impossible in terrestial labs, and to deduce information about the accuracy of GR and the state of matter at nuclear densities. The merger of compact objects produces a huge amount of neutron rich ejecta. It is within this ejecta that it is thought that around half of the heavy nuclei (>Fe) are produced.

The R-Process



  • Rapid neutron capture
  • $\tau_{\beta-decay} \gg \tau_{r-process}$
  • Decay of radioactive of isotopes
    re-heats the ejecta producing a
    radioactive glow
  • Luminosity peak is $10^3$x brighter than a supernova, hence the
    name kilonova
R-process stands for rapid neutron capture. When there is energetic and highly neutron rich matter, the timescale for neutron captures is far shorter than for beta decays. The result is neutron rich nuclei. Once the neutrons are used up (after a period of about 1 second), the isotopes begin to decay radioactively via fission, alpha and beta decay, forming the heavy stable elements. The energy released during these decays is either removed via neutrinos, or reinjected into the ejecta to heat it. The reheated ejecta then re-radiates thermally, proucing a radioactive glow on the order of a day/s similar to supernova glows but 10^3 more luminous at its peak. Hence, kilonova.

Observables



  • Re-heating results in unbound ejecta
  • Experience lulls or cut-offs of mass fall back on to central object
  • May lead to extended X-ray emission












[credit: Desai+ 18]

The reheating of the ejecta by radioactive decay has implications on the fall back of matter to the central object, affecting whether or not orbits are bound or unbound. If unbound, the mass accretion rate can experience temporal lulls to absolute cut-offs, potentially leading to gaps/lulls in the X-ray emission luminosity [Desai+18]. Constant mass fall back rates would expect a simple power law with no gaps in luminosity.

...and the simulations?



  • Nuclear reaction networks are slow!
  • It's implausible to expect full networks with GRHD/MHD simulations
  • We would like to have some model that captures this re-heating
Whilst we do have full nucear reaction networks, they are too expensive to simply employ in GRHD simulations. The aim of this project is to develop a simplified model of the heating rate that is a result of the decay of unstable r-process nuclei.


The dance of $\dot{Q}_{r}$ and $Y_e$



  • Can see that heating due to change in $Y_e$
  • Heating occurs over some timescale, $\tau_r$
Throughout this talk I will use the electron fraction as a proxy for how the system is heating. The change in electron fraction corresponds to a change in the composition of the nuclei, and thus will change the binding energy. This change in binding energy is responsible for the heating of the ejecta. We can see this by looking at the output of SkyNet. As Ye changes, there is an approximately constant heating rate which switches off as the electron fraction approaches the final value Yef.

Starting point


Take equations of motion...
$$ \begin{align} \nabla_{\alpha} (\rho u^{\alpha}) = 0 \\ \nabla_{\alpha} T^{\alpha \beta} = 0 \\ \nabla_{\alpha} (\rho Y_e u^{\alpha}) = 0 \end{align} $$


...and add r-process
$$ \begin{align} \nabla_{\alpha} (\rho u^{\alpha}) &= 0 \\ \nabla_{\alpha} T^{\alpha \beta} &= \dot{E}_{\nu} u^{\beta} \\ \nabla_{\alpha} (\rho Y_e u^{\alpha}) &= S_r \end{align} $$



Q: What to choose for $\dot{E}_{\nu}$ and $S_r$?

To start, we take the standard evolution equations of hydro+the electron fraction

Electron fraction source: $S_r$



  • Represents the change in $Y_e$ in time
  • Should be zero when there is no
    r-process



$$ \begin{align} S_r = \frac{Y_e^f - Y_e}{\tau_r} \end{align} $$


$$ \begin{align} \frac{\partial Y_e}{\partial t} \sim \frac{Y_e^f - Y_e}{\tau_r} \end{align} $$

Stress-energy source: $\dot{E}_{\nu}$



  • Represents the rate of energy loss through neutrinos
  • Total energy release due to change in specific binding energy, $\epsilon_B$
  • Energy loss rate, so occurs over some timescale



$\dot{E}_{\nu}=$$\ f_{\nu}$ $\ \rho_0 \frac{\partial \epsilon_B}{\partial Y_e}$ $\frac{Y_e^f - Y_e}{\tau_r }$


Free parameters are $Y_e^f$, $f_{\nu}$ and $\tau_r$.

How to determine binding energy, $\epsilon_B$?

Binding energy, $\epsilon_B$



  • Equal to internal energy density as $(\rho, T) \to 0$
  • Typically, tabulated EOSs untrusted beyond some bounds




  • Need to extend EOS to low densities and temperatures
  • Polytrope EOS: $\ \ p=\kappa \rho^{4/3}$
  • Binding energy is interpolated from trusted region of tabulated EOS
The specific binding energy is equal to the specific internal energy as the density and temperature approach zero. However, the tabulated EOS we use (LS220) is not well trusted for such low parameters, so we must extend our EOS such that it is useful in this regime. For this, we use the polytropic EOS, matched to the tabulated EOS along lines of constant entropy. With this, we can determine the thermodynamic properties by interpolating from regions in which LS220 is valid.
